# Pulse — Literature Review

## Why reasoning from a partial, evolving state is harder than reasoning from a finished, fully described one

When a system (human or model) is handed a completed match report, every event is already contextualized by the outcome — a red card in minute 30 "explains" the eventual loss, a substitution "explains" the late equalizer. This is hindsight bias: knowing the ending makes every prior event look more predictive of it than it actually was in real time. Reasoning from a frozen, partial state removes that scaffolding. The model has to weigh events (a goal, a card, a sub) against a genuinely open future, where several continuations are still plausible and most of the game's variance hasn't resolved yet. This is the same distinction the partially observable decision-making literature draws between operating over a belief state (a distribution over what might happen, updated as evidence arrives) versus reasoning post-hoc over a fully observed trajectory. The former requires calibrated uncertainty and correct updating; the latter only requires narrative coherence, which is much easier and can be right for the wrong reasons. A model can sound insightful describing a finished match while being unable to actually forecast one, because narrative fit and forecasting skill are not the same competence.

## Live win-probability models and why score + time-remaining is a strong baseline

In-play win-probability (WP) models—used in basketball, American football, and increasingly soccer—predict P(win/draw/loss) at any point in the match. The dominant approach is either (a) an empirical lookup table of historical win rates conditioned on (score differential, minute) buckets, (b) a logistic/multinomial regression or gradient-boosted model adding pre-match strength (Elo, market odds) and in-game events (red cards, penalties) as features, or (c) a generative approach that models goals as a time-inhomogeneous Poisson (or similar counting) process and simulates the remaining time to get an outcome distribution directly.

Score and time-remaining are hard to beat as a baseline because they already capture the two dominant sources of variance in a match: how big is the gap to close, and how much time is left to close it. In soccer specifically, goals are rare (~2.5-3 per match), so a one-goal lead is already a large, persistent signal, and the marginal information in any single additional event (a corner, a card, a chance) is small compared to that gap. This is exactly the bar Pulse must clear: a "team ahead now wins" or scoreline-and-minute base-rate baseline is not a strawman — it is close to the actual skill ceiling that many published in-play models struggle to beat by more than a small margin.

## Grading a forward prediction fairly: specificity, correctness, and calibration

A forecast is only checkable if it commits to something falsifiable. The forecasting evaluation literature (e.g., work following Tetlock's forecasting tournaments, and the broader proper-scoring-rule literature) draws a hard line between a prediction and a hedge: "the game will stay competitive" is not a prediction, because no outcome would contradict it. Grading forward predictions therefore requires two separate steps — first classify whether the claim is specific enough to resolve against reality (a gradable claim names a side, an outcome, or an event and a rough probability/confidence), and only then check whether it came true.

Once a prediction is gradable, correctness alone is not enough — a model that says "70%" on everything and is right 70% of the time by luck is not useful, and a model that is right but wildly overconfident is worse than one that is right and appropriately unsure. Proper scoring rules (Brier score, log loss) are used because they reward both being right and being honestly calibrated: they are minimized in expectation only when the stated probability matches the model's true belief. Calibration itself is usually checked separately from raw accuracy via reliability diagrams — bucket all predictions by stated confidence (e.g., all "~70%" claims across many matches) and check whether ~70% of them actually came true. This requires pooling many predictions, since a single match gives no signal about calibration, only about that one outcome. The Brier score decomposes into calibration + resolution (sharpness) components, which is useful for diagnosing whether a model that scores poorly is miscalibrated, just vague (low resolution, hedging near the base rate), or both.

## Simple in-play baselines

The literature (and betting-market practice) converges on a small set of baselines that are surprisingly hard to beat: (1) the leader holds on — whoever is ahead at the freeze point wins, with draws handled as a separate case; (2) historical base rates conditioned on scoreline and minute — e.g., across many historical matches, what fraction of 1-0-leads-at-minute-60 held to the final whistle; (3) a market/pre-match-strength-adjusted version of the above, when odds or Elo are available. These baselines matter for Pulse specifically because the project's real claim isn't "the model can predict football" — it's "the model adds situational reasoning beyond what score and time alone already tell you." That's only demonstrable if the model is compared against exactly this kind of baseline, not against random guessing.

## Momentum: real signal or storytelling bias?

Momentum is contested. In basketball, the classic "hot hand" literature (Gilovich, Vallone & Tversky, and later revisionist work) found that perceived streaks in shooting are largely illusory — clustered makes/misses are consistent with i.i.d. randomness, and observers overweight recent, salient sequences. In soccer specifically, there is some evidence of process-level momentum after certain shocks (e.g., a red card genuinely and durably shifts win probability, because it's a hard resource change, not a psychological one), but much weaker evidence for the vaguer "team is on top and it will snowball" narrative pundits invoke after a string of near-misses. The way to tell real momentum from storytelling bias is exactly the update-consistency test Pulse proposes: momentum is only a real, useful construct if flagging "team X is on top" at minute T predicts something checkable — that X creates the next clear chance, or scores next — at a rate above the base rate for the current scoreline/minute. If the model's momentum calls do no better than the base rate, "momentum" in its output is decoration, not signal.

## Here is the gap Pulse fills

Existing in-play win-probability work is almost entirely numeric — score, time, and structured event features feeding a regression or simulation model, evaluated purely on calibration and accuracy against a scoreline-and-minute baseline. It never asks whether a *reasoning* system can read an unfolding game the way an analyst does: name the current threats, justify a read of who's on top, and produce specific, checkable predictions in natural language, without seeing the outcome — and, critically, whether that read updates sensibly as new events (a red card, a goal) arrive within the same match. Pulse fills that gap: it takes the grading rigor of live win-probability modeling (freeze-and-reveal, proper baselines, calibration by pooling) and applies it to a language model's free-form situational judgment, testing both single-freeze accuracy and multi-freeze update consistency — a test no purely numeric WP model is even structured to attempt.
